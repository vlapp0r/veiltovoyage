## Start Earlier Than You Think: Your Planning Timeline

![Wedding planning timeline with calendar and checklist on a desk](/images/posts/2026-06-01-destination-wedding-planning-guide/image-2.jpg)
*Photo by Debby Hudson on Unsplash*


If there's one piece of advice that applies universally to destination weddings, it's this: **start earlier than feels necessary.** While a local wedding can come together in nine to twelve months, destination weddings typically need **twelve to twenty-four months** of lead time — especially if you're planning during a popular travel season or booking a high-demand venue.

Here's a rough framework to work from:

- **18–24 months out:** Choose your destination, set a rough budget, and begin researching venues and local wedding planners
- **12–18 months out:** Book your venue, hire a local wedding coordinator, and send save-the-dates to guests
- **9–12 months out:** Book accommodations, finalize your guest list, and handle legal requirements
- **6–9 months out:** Confirm vendors (photographer, caterer, florist, officiant), plan your rehearsal dinner
- **3–6 months out:** Finalize all contracts, arrange group travel or room blocks, apply for marriage licenses
- **1–3 months out:** Confirm all vendor details, create a wedding day timeline, pack everything twice

The earlier you begin, the more options you'll have — and the less you'll pay for last-minute bookings.

## Choosing the Right Destination (For More Than Just the Photos)

![Picturesque European countryside venue ideal for a destination wedding](/images/posts/2026-06-01-destination-wedding-planning-guide/image-3.jpg)
*Photo by Margit Knobloch on Unsplash*


Every destination wedding starts with a vision, but the best ones are chosen with practicality in mind too. Gorgeous scenery is great; a location that's impossible for your guests to reach is a problem.

When narrowing down your destination, think through these factors:

- **Guest accessibility:** Are there direct flights from where most of your guests live? What's the average travel cost they'd be facing?
- **Legal requirements:** Some countries make it very easy for foreigners to legally marry there; others require weeks of paperwork, residency periods, or religious documentation. Research this early.
- **Season and weather:** That dreamy Italian hillside in July might also be 100°F with crowds. Look at shoulder seasons for better weather, better prices, and more intimate experiences.
- **Your personal connection:** Does the destination mean something to you as a couple? A place you've visited together or always dreamed of going will always feel more authentic than a trending backdrop.
- **Vendor availability:** Are there experienced local wedding vendors who speak your language (literally and figuratively)?

Popular destination wedding hotspots for 2026 include the Greek islands, Mexico's Riviera Maya, Portugal's Algarve coast, the mountains of Colorado and Vermont, and coastal Italy. But honestly? The best destination is the one that feels like *you*.

## Budgeting for a Destination Wedding: What to Expect

Let's talk money — because destination weddings can range from surprisingly affordable to jaw-droppingly expensive, depending on your choices.

The good news: because guest lists are typically smaller (30–80 people is common), per-person costs can actually balance out or even favor a destination celebration. The not-so-good news: travel costs, international vendor fees, and currency fluctuations can add up fast.

**Budget line items to plan for:**

- Venue rental fees (often higher than local venues, but it includes ambiance you can't recreate)
- Local wedding coordinator or planner (non-negotiable — more on this below)
- Travel and accommodation for the couple and wedding party
- Guest accommodation blocks (you typically won't pay for these, but you'll organize them)
- Vendors: photographer, videographer, florist, caterer, officiant, entertainment
- Legal and administrative fees for marriage documentation
- A legal ceremony at home if required (some couples do a small courthouse ceremony locally)
- Gratuities and tips for local vendors (research customs in your destination country)

**Pro tip:** Build in a 10–15% contingency fund. Currency rates shift, international shipping costs more than expected, and you *will* want a few extras you didn't originally plan for.

## Why You Absolutely Need a Local Wedding Coordinator

We can't stress this enough: **hire a local wedding coordinator.** This is the one place in your destination wedding budget where cutting corners will cost you more than the savings are worth.

A coordinator who is based in or deeply familiar with your destination brings:

- **Vetted vendor relationships** — they know who's reliable, who photographs beautifully, and who to avoid
- **Language skills and cultural knowledge** — navigating contracts and customs in another country is genuinely hard
- **Crisis management** — when the flowers don't arrive or the weather turns, they have a plan B (and sometimes a plan C)
- **Time zone coverage** — they're available during business hours in your destination country, not just yours
- **Permitting and logistics knowledge** — beaches, historical sites, and vineyards often require special permissions

Look for coordinators with international wedding certifications or strong portfolios with real client reviews. ## Navigating the Legal Side of Marrying Abroad

Here's the section that surprises most couples: **getting legally married in another country is not always straightforward.** Requirements vary enormously by country, and getting this wrong can mean your marriage isn't legally recognized at home.

Common legal requirements for marrying abroad include:

- **Proof of single status** — some countries require an official document stating you are legally free to marry
- **Residency requirements** — certain destinations require you to be present in the country for a set number of days before the ceremony
- **Translated and apostilled documents** — birth certificates, passports, and divorce decrees may need official translation and a legal stamp called an apostille
- **Religious or civil ceremony requirements** — some countries only legally recognize religious ceremonies (or vice versa)

**Our honest advice:** Research your specific destination's requirements at least 12 months in advance, and consider consulting with a local lawyer or your destination country's embassy. Many couples also choose to have a small, legally binding ceremony at home (at the courthouse or with a local officiant) and treat the destination celebration as their "real" wedding ceremony — the one with all the emotion, guests, and beauty.

## Creating the Guest Experience (Because They Traveled for You)

Your guests are flying hours to celebrate your love. That deserves acknowledgment — and a little extra thoughtfulness in your planning.

Here's how to make sure your guests feel taken care of:

- **Organize accommodation blocks:** Negotiate group rates at a hotel near your venue and share the details clearly. Make it easy for guests to book.
- **Create a wedding website:** Include travel tips, local recommendations, packing lists, visa requirements, and a full schedule of events
- **Plan a welcome event:** A casual welcome dinner or cocktail hour the night before allows guests to connect and feel oriented before the big day
- **Provide transportation:** Arrange shuttles between hotels and the venue so guests aren't stressing about navigation in an unfamiliar place
- **Assemble welcome bags:** Small bags with local snacks, a destination guide, water, and a heartfelt note go a long way
- **Build in free time:** Not everyone wants a scheduled activity every day — give guests breathing room to explore

Remember: they chose to be there. Honor that by making the experience feel as effortless and joyful as possible.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How many guests typically attend a destination wedding?**
A: Most destination weddings have between 20 and 80 guests — significantly smaller than the average 130-person local wedding. This is actually one of the best parts. Smaller guest lists mean more meaningful interactions, lower per-head catering costs, and an overall more intimate atmosphere. Don't be discouraged if some guests can't make the trip; most couples also host a casual celebration at home afterward for those who couldn't attend.

**Q: Do I need to hire a separate wedding planner if my venue has a coordinator?**
A: Venue coordinators are invaluable for managing the space itself, but their job typically ends at the venue's front door. A dedicated wedding planner works on your behalf across all vendors, travel logistics, and the full guest experience. For destination weddings especially, having your own planner — even a partial-planning consultant — is well worth the investment.

**Q: How do I handle currency and payments for international vendors?**
A: Many international vendors require deposits in their local currency via international bank transfer. Use a service like Wise (formerly TransferWise) to avoid excessive bank fees and unfavorable exchange rates. Always get contracts in writing, confirm payment schedules before signing, and keep records of every transaction.

**Q: What happens if a guest cancels last minute?**
A: This is one of the emotional realities of destination weddings — travel complications happen. Be clear on your vendor contracts about minimum guest counts for catering, and set your RSVP deadline early enough to adjust accordingly. Try not to take late cancellations personally; most guests genuinely want to be there and are disappointed when life gets in the way.
