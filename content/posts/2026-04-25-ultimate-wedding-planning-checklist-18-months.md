## 18 Months Out: Lay the Foundation

The moment that ring is on your finger, the clock starts ticking on venue availability. Premium venues and sought-after photographers often book up 18–24 months in advance, so this phase is all about the big-ticket decisions.

**What to tackle first:**

- **Set your overall budget.** Sit down with your partner (and any family contributors) and establish a realistic total number. Everything else flows from this.
- **Estimate your guest count.** Your number of guests drives venue size, catering costs, and invitation quantities. Even a rough number helps enormously at this stage.
- **Choose your wedding date** — or at least a season. Flexibility here gives you more venue options and potentially better pricing.
- **Research and book your venue.** Visit at least 3–4 options before signing anything.
- **Start your wedding registry.** Yes, this early! It also comes with a free wedding website — one less thing to sort out later.
- **Hire your photographer and videographer.** These vendors are the *fastest* to book out, and you cannot get your wedding day back on film.
- **Get engaged-party photos done** if your family is expecting them for save-the-dates.

## 12–15 Months Out: Build Your Dream Team

Now that the foundation is set, it's time to fill in the vendor lineup and nail down the bigger vision for your day.

**Key tasks for this stage:**

- **Book your caterer** (if not included with the venue).
- **Hire your officiant.** Check their availability, meet with them, and discuss the ceremony style you want.
- **Book your florist.** Bring inspiration images and be upfront about your floral budget.
- **Choose your wedding planner or day-of coordinator.** Even if you're planning most things yourself, a day-of coordinator is worth every penny.
- **Select and book your band or DJ.**
- **Start dress shopping.** Wedding gowns typically take 4–6 months to arrive *plus* time for alterations. Don't cut this one close.
- **Book hair and makeup artists** for a trial run — the good ones fill up fast.
- **Send save-the-dates** for destination or holiday-weekend weddings. For local weddings, you can wait until 10–12 months out.
- **Begin honeymoon research.** Popular destinations and peak-season hotel blocks book up, too. Dreaming early = better deals.

## 9–12 Months Out: The Details Start Taking Shape

By now, the major vendors are locked in. This phase is where the wedding truly starts to feel *real* — and where thoughtful planning pays off big time.

**Focus on:**

- **Finalize your guest list** down to the name-and-address level.
- **Design and order your invitations.** Plan to mail them 8–10 weeks before the wedding, so ordering 10–12 months out gives you plenty of breathing room for revisions.
- **Book hotel room blocks** for out-of-town guests.
- **Plan your ceremony details.** Music selections, readings, vows, processional order — start thinking through these now.
- **Research wedding insurance.** Yes, it's a thing, and yes, it's worth it. Policies typically need to be purchased well in advance.
- **Continue building your Zola registry.** You'll want a mix of price points, including a few experiences and group gifting options. Zola's registry manager makes it easy to track what's been purchased and send thank-you notes in one place.
- **Schedule your first cake tasting.** (One of the more delicious to-do items on this list.)
- **Choose your wedding party attire** so everyone has time to order and alter.

## 6–9 Months Out: Confirm and Commit

You're officially past the halfway mark. Things can feel a little overwhelming here — there are a lot of moving pieces — but this is also when the vision really crystallizes.

**What to check off:**

- **Mail your invitations** (8–10 weeks before the wedding date, so aim to have them ready now).
- **Book rehearsal dinner venue and vendors.**
- **Finalize your menu** with your caterer, including dietary restrictions from your RSVP responses.
- **Purchase wedding rings.**
- **Apply for your marriage license** (check your local requirements — some have waiting periods).
- **Finalize transportation** — getting the wedding party to the ceremony and getting you two to the reception in style.
- **Schedule and complete hair and makeup trials.**
- **Create a wedding day timeline** and share it with your photographer, coordinator, and wedding party.
- **Plan welcome bags** if you're hosting out-of-town guests.

---

## 1–3 Months Out: The Final Stretch

Deep breath. You've done most of the heavy lifting. This phase is about tightening up details and making sure everyone is on the same page.

**Final tasks to complete:**

- **Confirm all vendors** in writing — send them the finalized timeline and your contact sheet.
- **Seat your guests** using your finalized RSVP count. Give yourself a buffer of 1–2 weeks past your RSVP deadline before doing this.
- **Prepare vendor payments and tip envelopes.** Calculate gratuities in advance so it's not a scramble on the day.
- **Break in your wedding shoes.** Wear them around the house. Your future self will thank you.
- **Write your personal vows** if you're doing them — don't leave this to the night before.
- **Pack your honeymoon essentials** and check passport expiration dates.
- **Delegate day-of tasks** to a trusted bridesmaid, groomsman, or family member. You should not be answering vendor calls on your wedding day.
- **Do a final dress fitting.**
- **Create an emergency kit** (safety pins, stain remover pen, pain reliever, hair ties — you know the drill).

---

## The Week Of: Breathe, Delegate, and Be Present

The week of your wedding is not the time to start planning — it's the time to *celebrate*. If you've followed this checklist, nearly everything is handled.

**This week, focus on:**

- Attending your rehearsal and rehearsal dinner.
- Getting a massage or doing whatever helps you decompress.
- Charging all devices and confirming pick-up and drop-off times with drivers.
- Handing over your vendor binder or contact sheet to your coordinator or point person.
- Getting enough sleep before your wedding day — genuinely underrated advice.
- **Putting your phone down** and being present with the people you love most.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Is 18 months really enough time to plan a wedding?**
A: For most couples, 18 months is actually a *generous* amount of time — and a wonderful gift to yourself. It allows you to research vendors carefully, avoid rushing into decisions, and spread costs over a longer period. If your dream venue is a popular one or you're planning a large celebration, having that 18-month runway can be the difference between getting your first choice and settling.

**Q: What's the very first thing I should do after getting engaged?**
A: Before you post on Instagram, have an honest money conversation with your partner about your overall budget. Every other decision — venue, guest count, catering, florals — flows from that number. It's not the most romantic task, but it sets you up for a stress-free planning journey.

**Q: When should I set up my wedding registry?**
A: As soon as possible — ideally within the first month or two of your engagement. Family members start asking immediately, and sending save-the-dates without registry information leaves guests without guidance. Setting up your registry early also lets you add items thoughtfully over time rather than in a frantic rush.

**Q: How do I stay on budget throughout 18 months of planning?**
A: Track every expense in a dedicated spreadsheet from day one, and revisit it monthly. Categorize spending into buckets (venue, catering, florals, photography, attire, etc.) and allocate percentages based on your priorities. If something goes over, something else needs to come down — and being honest about that early keeps small overages from becoming big ones.
